Here it is, in one sentence:

The whole idea

A raw AI language model out of training can only continue text. RLHF is part of the process that turns that text-continuer into something that answers you helpfully — by learning from humans which answers are better.

1. A base model just continues text

A language model is trained on enormous amounts of internet text with one simple objective: predict the next word. Do that billions of times and you get a base model (also called a pretrained model). It is startlingly knowledgeable — but it does not "answer questions." It just continues whatever text you give it, the way autocomplete would.

Give a strong base model the prompt "The president of the United States in 2006 was" and it produces a sprawl of plausible internet text:

George W. Bush, the governor of Florida in 2006 was Jeb Bush, and
John McCain was an Arizona senator... September 1 -- U.S. President
Bush signs an executive order... 2009 was a common year starting on
Thursday of the Gregorian calendar...

It is not wrong, exactly — it is doing its job: continuing the text with more text that looks like the internet. It just isn't useful.

2. Post-training turns it into an assistant

Everything we do after that giant pretraining run, to make the model useful, is called post-training. After post-training, the same question gets a clean answer:

George W. Bush was the president of the United States in 2006.
He served two terms, from January 20, 2001 to January 20, 2009.

Post-training has three main tools. This is the map for our entire course — keep it in your head:

┌─────────────┐ ① SFT ┌──────────────┐ ②③ RLHF ┌────────────────┐ │ BASE MODEL │ ─────────────▶ │ CAN ANSWER │ ─────────────▶ │ HELPFUL, │ │ (completes │ show it Q→A │ (right │ learn from │ PREFERRED │ │ text) │ examples │ format) │ human prefs │ ASSISTANT │ └─────────────┘ └──────────────┘ └────────────────┘
  1. SFT (instruction / supervised fine-tuning) — show the model lots of example question → good answer pairs so it learns the format of being a helpful assistant. This teaches the shape of an answer.
  2. Reward modeling — collect humans' judgments of which answers are better, and train a separate model (the reward model) to predict that "goodness" as a single number.
  3. RL optimization — let the model generate answers, score them with the reward model, and nudge the model to produce more of the high-scoring kind.

Steps ② and ③ together are RLHF: Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback. SFT teaches the model a good answer; RLHF teaches it what makes one answer better than another.

Analogy · the F1 car

Nathan Lambert (the book's author) compares the base model to a Formula 1 chassis. The chassis sets the ceiling of what's possible, but teams win or lose the season on everything they bolt on afterward — aerodynamics, tuning, systems. Post-training is that tuning: it doesn't build a new car, it extracts the performance already latent in the chassis. The book calls this the elicitation theory of post-training.

3. Why not just write rules?

Why bother with human feedback at all — why not just tell the model the rules for a good answer? Because "a good answer" is mostly unspecifiable. What makes a reply feel warm, well-formatted, helpful, or safe is something humans recognize instantly but struggle to write down. RLHF's founding question was exactly this:

"Can we solve hard problems using only basic preference signals — humans saying this one is better than that one — to guide the optimization?"

That word preference is the heart of it. Instead of grading an answer in isolation, we compare two and ask a human (or later, another AI) which is better. Learning from comparisons like this is called a contrastive signal, and it is the recurring theme of this whole book.

Watch out

A common myth (you'll hear it as "instruction tuning is enough") is that RLHF only adds surface-level "style." It does shape style — but modern post-training also unlocks real behaviors like step-by-step reasoning. Don't file RLHF under "cosmetic."

Close, but RLHF is specifically the reward-model step plus the RL-optimization step. SFT/pretraining come before it.

3. Why use human preferences instead of hand-written rules?

Yes. The quality we want is unspecifiable in rules, but humans can reliably say which of two answers is better — a contrastive signal.

The core reason is specifiability: we can't write down what makes an answer "good," but humans can compare two and pick the better one.
