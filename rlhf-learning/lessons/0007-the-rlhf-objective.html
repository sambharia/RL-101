The RLHF Objective — Inside the Math

You know the formula from Lesson 6. Now we open it up: what does each piece actually compute for a real response, token by token? By the end, you'll be able to calculate the objective by hand — and you'll see the beautiful closed-form solution hiding inside it.

Phase 1 — The RLHF big picture Prerequisites: Lessons 4 (probability), 6 (pipeline) ~35 min

Warm-up: pull it back from memory

SR1 (Lesson 6) — What are the three steps of the RLHF pipeline? What is the training signal for each?

(1) SFT: train on (prompt, response) pairs using cross-entropy loss ($-\log P$ of correct token). (2) Reward Model: train on human preference comparisons — winning response should score higher. (3) RL: generate responses, score with reward model, maximize $\mathbb{E}[R] - \beta \text{KL}$.

SR2 (Lesson 6) — What is the reference policy $\pi_\text{ref}$, and why does the objective include it?

$\pi_\text{ref}$ is the SFT model, frozen — never updated during RL training. It's included in the KL penalty to prevent the policy from drifting too far from natural language and hacking the reward model.

SR3 (Lesson 4) — What does $\log P(x)$ tell you? What happens to it when $P(x)$ is small?

$\log P(x)$ is the log-probability of event $x$. When $P(x)$ is small (unlikely), $\log P(x)$ is a large negative number. When $P(x) = 1$, $\log P(x) = 0$. Log-probs turn products into sums, which is why we use them for sequences of tokens.

1. The formula you already know — but can you compute it?

In Lesson 6, you learned the RLHF objective:

$$\max_{\pi}\; \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D},\, y \sim \pi(\cdot\,|\,x)}\!\Big[\,R(x,y)\,\Big] \;-\; \beta\,\text{KL}\!\big[\,\pi \;\|\; \pi_{\text{ref}}\,\big]$$

You can name every symbol. But here's the test: suppose the model generates a 5-token response. Can you actually calculate each piece? What number does $\text{KL}[\pi \| \pi_\text{ref}]$ evaluate to? How does the model compute it, token by token?

That's what this lesson is about. We'll open up each piece, compute it on a concrete example, and discover a surprising result hiding inside the formula.

2. First: the probability of a full response

Before we can touch KL divergence, we need one fact: how does a language model assign a probability to an entire response?

You already know from Lesson 2 that LMs generate one token at a time. At each step, the model sees everything so far and produces a probability distribution over the next token. The probability of the full response is the product of all these per-token probabilities:

Probability of a response — the chain rule
$$\pi(y \mid x) = \prod_{t=1}^{T} \pi(y_t \mid x, y_1, \ldots, y_{t-1})$$
$y = (y_1, \ldots, y_T)$ The full response — a sequence of $T$ tokens.
$x$ The prompt.
$\pi(y_t \mid x, y_{ The probability the model assigns to token $y_t$, given the prompt and all previous tokens.

This is just the chain rule of probability — and it's exactly how microGPT generates text: pick a token, append it, feed it back, repeat.

Taking the log turns the product into a sum (from Lesson 4):

$$\log \pi(y \mid x) = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \log \pi(y_t \mid x, y_{This is the log-probability of the entire response. Each term is one number that the model already computes during its forward pass — it's the log of the softmax output for the token it chose. Nothing new to calculate.

3. KL divergence: measuring how different two models are

Here's the question the KL term answers: how much has the policy changed from the reference model?

In Lesson 6, we said the KL is built from log-ratios: $\log \frac{\pi(y)}{\pi_\text{ref}(y)}$. Let's now be precise about what that means.

KL divergence in one sentence

KL divergence measures how "surprised" you would be if you thought the data came from distribution $Q$ but it actually came from distribution $P$. It's always $\geq 0$, and equals $0$ only when the two distributions are identical.

KL divergence — the general formula

For two distributions $P$ and $Q$ over the same set of outcomes:

$$\text{KL}[P \| Q] = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim P}\!\left[\log \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}\right] = \sum_{x} P(x) \log \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}$$

Read it as: "sample from $P$, and measure how much $P$ and $Q$ disagree on average."

$\log \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}$ The log-ratio for one outcome $x$. Positive when $P$ assigns more probability than $Q$. Negative when $Q$ assigns more.
$\mathbb{E}_{x \sim P}[\cdot]$ Average over outcomes, weighted by $P$. Outcomes that $P$ thinks are likely matter more.

Two key properties you should keep in your head:

  1. Always $\geq 0$. KL divergence is never negative. If you compute a negative number, you made a math error.
  2. Not symmetric. $\text{KL}[P \| Q] \neq \text{KL}[Q \| P]$ in general. The order matters: the first distribution ($P$) is the one you sample from.
Why log-ratios and not just differences?

You might wonder: why $\log \frac{P}{Q}$ instead of $P - Q$? Two reasons. First, probabilities are multiplicative — a sequence of tokens has probability that's a product, so ratios are the natural comparison. Second, the log-ratio has a nice interpretation: it's the "evidence" that the sample came from $P$ rather than $Q$. A log-ratio of $+2$ means $P$ is about $e^2 \approx 7\times$ more likely than $Q$ to have produced this outcome.

4. KL for language models: the token-by-token trick

Here's where it gets concrete. In RLHF, $P = \pi$ (the policy being trained) and $Q = \pi_\text{ref}$ (the frozen SFT model). The "outcomes" are full responses — entire sequences of tokens.

But we don't need to think about probabilities of whole responses. Thanks to the chain rule from Section 2, the KL over full responses decomposes into a sum of per-token KLs:

Derivation — KL decomposes token by token
Start with the log-ratio of the full response:
$$\log \frac{\pi(y \mid x)}{\pi_\text{ref}(y \mid x)}$$
Expand both using the chain rule:
$$= \log \frac{\prod_{t=1}^{T} \pi(y_t \mid x, y_{
Log of a ratio of products = sum of log-ratios:
$$= \sum_{t=1}^{T} \log \frac{\pi(y_t \mid x, y_{
Each term is one number:

At position $t$, run both $\pi$ and $\pi_\text{ref}$ on the same input $(x, y_{

This is the key insight of this section: to compute the KL between two language models on a response, you just run both models on the same input, look up the probability each assigns to each token, and sum the log-ratios.

Why this matters practically

During RLHF training, you already have to run the policy $\pi$ to generate the response. To compute the KL, you also run the reference model $\pi_\text{ref}$ on the same response (but you don't update $\pi_\text{ref}$ — it's frozen). At each token position, both models output a probability. You take the log-ratio. Sum them up. That's the KL. No extra theory, no complicated integrals — just two forward passes and some subtraction in log-space.

The policy has drifted slightly from the reference — it assigns a bit more probability to this response than $\pi_\text{ref}$ does. The drift is small (0.32), so the KL penalty will be mild.
The log-ratio can be negative at individual tokens

Notice token 3 ("things") has a negative log-ratio: the reference model actually assigns more probability to it than the policy does. That's fine — individual tokens can go either way. What matters is the sum over all tokens. When you take the expectation over all possible responses (not just this one), the KL is guaranteed to be $\geq 0$. For any single response, the sum of log-ratios can be negative — that just means the reference model likes this particular response more than the policy does.

5. The full objective, piece by piece

Now we can write the RLHF objective in a form where every piece is something you can compute for a single response:

The RLHF objective — expanded
$$\max_\pi \;\mathbb{E}_{x,y}\!\left[\; R(x,y) \;-\; \beta \sum_{t=1}^{T} \log \frac{\pi(y_t \mid x, y_{For a single response $y$ to prompt $x$, the thing inside the expectation is:

$$\underbrace{R(x,y)}_{\text{reward model score}} \;-\; \beta \underbrace{\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log \frac{\pi(y_t \mid x, y_{

This is the per-response score — the number the RL optimizer tries to make large. It has two parts pulling in opposite directions:

  • The reward $R(x,y)$ wants to go up — generate better responses.
  • The KL penalty (the sum of log-ratios, scaled by $\beta$) pushes back whenever the policy diverges from the reference. The further you drift, the bigger this penalty.
The effective reward

Papers often define the effective reward (or "KL-penalized reward") as a single number per response:

$$r_\text{eff}(x, y) = R(x,y) - \beta \sum_{t=1}^{T} \log \frac{\pi(y_t \mid x, y_{The RLHF objective is then simply: maximize $\mathbb{E}[r_\text{eff}]$. This is what the RL algorithm (PPO, GRPO, etc.) actually optimizes — it treats $r_\text{eff}$ as the reward signal, not just $R$.

Worked example: computing the effective reward

Using our "Explain gravity" example from Section 4:

Reward model score:
$R = 7.2$ (the reward model thinks this is a good response)
Sum of per-token log-ratios:
$0.32$ (computed above — small drift)
Let $\beta = 0.1$:
$r_\text{eff} = 7.2 - 0.1 \times 0.32 = 7.2 - 0.032 = 7.168$
Verdict:
The KL penalty barely touched the reward. This response is close to what the SFT model would say, so it gets almost the full reward. Good.

Now suppose a degenerate response: ["great", "great", "great", "great", "!"]

Reward model score:
$R = 4.5$ (the word "great" helped a bit, but it's garbage)
Sum of per-token log-ratios:
$6.8$ (the SFT model would almost never produce this exact sequence — huge drift)
Effective reward:
$r_\text{eff} = 4.5 - 0.1 \times 6.8 = 4.5 - 0.68 = 3.82$
With a higher $\beta = 1.0$:
$r_\text{eff} = 4.5 - 1.0 \times 6.8 = 4.5 - 6.8 = -2.3$
Verdict:
With $\beta = 1.0$, the penalty overwhelms the reward — the effective reward is negative. The model learns to avoid this response. Higher $\beta$ means stricter enforcement.

6. The $\beta$ knob: what it controls

$\beta$ is the single most important hyperparameter in RLHF. It controls the tradeoff between two forces:

Low $\beta$ (e.g., 0.01)High $\beta$ (e.g., 1.0 or higher)
KL penalty is weak — the model can drift far from $\pi_\text{ref}$ KL penalty is strong — the model must stay close to $\pi_\text{ref}$
Reward is the dominant signal Staying close to SFT is the dominant signal
Risk: reward hacking, mode collapse Risk: model barely improves over SFT

In practice, $\beta$ is tuned empirically. The InstructGPT paper used values in the range of 0.01–0.1. Too low and the model exploits the reward model; too high and you might as well have just used SFT.

7. The beautiful result: the optimal policy

Here's something remarkable. If you could perfectly optimize the RLHF objective — no function approximation, no gradient noise, just pure math — what policy $\pi^*$ would you get?

It turns out there's a closed-form answer. Let's derive it.

Derivation — the optimal policy
Step 1: Write the objective for a fixed prompt $x$

For a single prompt, we want to maximize over all possible policies $\pi(\cdot | x)$:

$$\sum_{y} \pi(y|x) \Big[ R(x,y) - \beta \log \frac{\pi(y|x)}{\pi_\text{ref}(y|x)} \Big]$$

This is the expectation $\mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi}[\cdot]$ written as an explicit sum over all responses.

Step 2: Rearrange the log-ratio

Rewrite $\log \frac{\pi(y|x)}{\pi_\text{ref}(y|x)}$ as $\log \pi(y|x) - \log \pi_\text{ref}(y|x)$. The objective becomes:

$$\sum_{y} \pi(y|x) \Big[ R(x,y) + \beta \log \pi_\text{ref}(y|x) - \beta \log \pi(y|x) \Big]$$
Step 3: Recognize the shape

The term $-\beta \sum_y \pi(y|x) \log \pi(y|x)$ is $\beta$ times the entropy of $\pi$ — it rewards spread-out distributions. The rest pulls $\pi$ toward responses with high $R(x,y) + \beta \log \pi_\text{ref}(y|x)$.

This is a classic entropy-regularized optimization problem. The solution is a Boltzmann distribution (the same shape as the softmax you already know):

Step 4: The answer
$$\pi^*(y \mid x) = \frac{1}{Z(x)}\; \pi_\text{ref}(y \mid x)\; \exp\!\left(\frac{R(x,y)}{\beta}\right)$$

where $Z(x) = \sum_{y'} \pi_\text{ref}(y' \mid x)\, \exp\!\left(\frac{R(x,y')}{\beta}\right)$ is a normalizing constant (the partition function — it makes the probabilities sum to 1).

The optimal RLHF policy
$$\pi^*(y \mid x) \;\propto\; \pi_\text{ref}(y \mid x) \cdot \exp\!\left(\frac{R(x,y)}{\beta}\right)$$

Read this formula in plain English: the optimal policy takes the reference model's distribution and tilts it toward high-reward responses. Specifically:

  • Start with $\pi_\text{ref}(y|x)$ — how likely the SFT model thinks response $y$ is.
  • Multiply by $\exp(R/\beta)$ — boost responses proportionally to their reward. Higher reward = bigger boost.
  • Divide by $Z(x)$ — normalize so probabilities sum to 1.
Why this is beautiful

This formula tells you exactly what RLHF is doing: it's reweighting the reference distribution by reward. It's not learning from scratch — it's adjusting what the SFT model already knows. Responses the reference model would never produce ($\pi_\text{ref} \approx 0$) stay suppressed even if they score well. Responses the reference model already likes get a further boost if the reward model agrees.

The $\beta$ parameter controls how aggressively you reweight. As $\beta \to \infty$, $\exp(R/\beta) \to 1$ for all responses, so $\pi^* \to \pi_\text{ref}$ — you don't change anything. As $\beta \to 0$, all probability mass concentrates on the single highest-reward response — pure reward maximization.

You've seen this shape before

The formula $\pi^*(y|x) \propto \exp(\text{score}(y)/\text{temperature})$ is exactly softmax with temperature — the same operation microGPT uses to sample tokens! In Lesson 2, the temperature parameter controlled how "peaked" the distribution was. Here, $\beta$ plays the same role: it's the temperature of a softmax over full responses, weighted by reward. The connection is deep: softmax over scores is the solution to any "maximize expected score minus entropy" problem.

8. A formula you'll see everywhere: extracting the reward

The optimal policy formula can be rearranged to express the reward in terms of probabilities. Take the log of both sides of $\pi^*(y|x) = \frac{1}{Z(x)} \pi_\text{ref}(y|x) \exp(R/\beta)$:

$$R(x,y) = \beta \log \frac{\pi^*(y \mid x)}{\pi_\text{ref}(y \mid x)} + \beta \log Z(x)$$
Why this matters: the bridge to DPO

This rearrangement says: the reward is just the log-ratio between the optimal policy and the reference, up to a constant. This is the key insight behind DPO (Direct Preference Optimization), which you'll see in Lesson 12. DPO plugs this formula into the reward model's loss function and eliminates the reward model entirely — training the policy directly on preferences. You don't need to understand DPO yet, but notice: this one equation is the bridge.

Self-check

Q1 — To compute the KL between $\pi$ and $\pi_\text{ref}$ for a specific response, what do you need?

Correct. The KL for a specific response is the sum of per-token log-ratios: $\sum_t \log \pi(y_t|\ldots) - \log \pi_\text{ref}(y_t|\ldots)$. You need both models' log-probs at each token position — nothing else.

The KL for a specific response is computed from per-token log-probabilities from both models. At each token position, subtract the reference model's log-prob from the policy's log-prob, then sum. No reward scores, no gradients, no vocabulary size needed.

Q2 — In the optimal policy $\pi^*(y|x) \propto \pi_\text{ref}(y|x) \cdot \exp(R/\beta)$, what happens as $\beta \to 0$?

Correct. As $\beta \to 0$, $\exp(R/\beta)$ grows exponentially for the highest-reward response and shrinks for everything else — all mass concentrates on the argmax. This is pure reward maximization with no KL constraint. In practice, this leads to reward hacking.

When $\beta \to 0$, $\exp(R/\beta)$ magnifies reward differences enormously. The response with the highest reward gets $\exp(\text{huge})$ while others get $\exp(\text{less huge})$ — in the limit, all probability goes to the argmax. When $\beta \to \infty$, the opposite happens: $\exp(R/\beta) \to 1$ for all, so $\pi^* \to \pi_\text{ref}$.

Q3 (hard) — A response has reward $R = 6.0$, and its per-token log-ratio sum is $-0.5$ (negative — the reference model assigns more probability to this response). With $\beta = 0.2$, what is the effective reward, and what does the negative log-ratio sum mean?

Correct. $r_\text{eff} = 6.0 - 0.2 \times (-0.5) = 6.0 + 0.1 = 6.1$. When the policy assigns less probability than the reference to a response, the log-ratio sum is negative, and subtracting a negative number adds to the reward. It's a "bonus" for being conservative — the model stayed closer to the reference than the reference itself, and the objective rewards that.

Compute: $r_\text{eff} = R - \beta \times \text{log-ratio sum} = 6.0 - 0.2 \times (-0.5) = 6.0 + 0.1 = 6.1$. The log-ratio sum for a single response can be negative (the reference likes this response more than the policy does), and when it is, the penalty term becomes a bonus. The KL divergence as an expectation over all responses is always $\geq 0$, but for any individual response the log-ratio sum can go either way.
