How to Read Math Formulas

A focused crash course on the notation you'll see in RLHF — starting from the formula that triggered this.

The formula you were looking at

$$L = -\log\, P(\,y_{\text{correct}} \mid \text{context};\, \theta\,)$$

Five pieces. Let's take each one apart.

Breaking it down, piece by piece

Piece 1 — $L$

L = Loss. Just a name for "the number we're trying to minimise." Authors could have called it anything. $L$ is convention for loss.

Piece 2 — $P(\cdots)$

P = probability. A number between 0 and 1. Always. $P(\text{something}) = 0.7$ means "70% chance of something."

In ML, $P$ usually means "the model assigns this probability." It's the output of softmax from Lesson 2.

Piece 3 — the vertical bar $\mid$ (the conditional bar)

Read as "given". Everything to the left of $\mid$ is what you're computing the probability of. Everything to the right is what you already know / are conditioning on.

$P(\text{rain} \mid \text{clouds})$ = "probability of rain, given that I know it's cloudy."

$P(y_{\text{correct}} \mid \text{context})$ = "probability the model assigns to the correct next token, given all the previous tokens."

Piece 4 — the semicolon $;\, \theta$

The semicolon means "parameterised by" — it's saying which model/version you're using. $\theta$ (theta) is the collection of all the model's parameters (weights).

$P(y \mid x;\, \theta)$ means "the probability this specific model (with these specific weights) assigns to $y$ given $x$." If you change $\theta$, the probability changes.

Piece 5 — $-\log(\cdots)$

The negative log. This is the key operation. See the full section below.

The $\log$ — what it does and why

If you've forgotten logs: $\log(x)$ is the inverse of the exponential. But you don't need to remember that. You need three facts:

Fact 1 — log turns multiplication into addition
$$\log(A \times B) = \log A + \log B$$

This is why it's used everywhere in ML. Probabilities multiply across steps. Computers can't multiply millions of tiny numbers (they underflow to zero). The log turns the product into a sum — safe and easy.

Fact 2 — log of a small probability is a large negative number
Probability $P$$\log P$ (natural log)$-\log P$ (the loss)
1.0 (perfect)00 — no loss
0.5−0.690.69 — small loss
0.1−2.302.30 — real loss
0.01−4.604.60 — bad
0.001−6.916.91 — very bad
0.0001−9.219.21 — terrible

Pattern: when the model assigns near-zero probability to the correct answer, the loss explodes. When it assigns near-1, loss → 0. That's the entire design.

Fact 3 — why the negative sign

$\log P$ is always negative (because $P$ is between 0 and 1, and $\log$ of anything less than 1 is negative). We want loss to be a positive number — higher = worse. So we flip the sign: $-\log P$ is always positive, and smaller probabilities → larger losses.

General rules for reading any ML formula

Symbol / PatternWhat it meansExample
$\theta$ (theta) Model parameters (all the weights collected into one label) $\pi_\theta$ = the model (policy) with these weights
$\mid$ (vertical bar) "given" — probability of left side, knowing right side is fixed $P(y \mid x)$ = prob of $y$ given $x$
$;$ (semicolon) "parameterised by" — which model/version you're using $P(y \mid x;\, \theta)$ = prob from this specific model
Subscript $y_t$ The $t$-th item in a sequence (indexing) $y_3$ = the 3rd generated token
$\mathbb{E}[\cdots]$ Expected value — weighted average over all possibilities $\mathbb{E}[r(y)]$ = average reward the policy earns
$\sum$ (sigma) Sum over a range of values $\sum_{i=1}^{N} x_i = x_1 + x_2 + \cdots + x_N$
$\prod$ (pi) Product over a range (multiply, not add) $\prod_{t=1}^{T} P(y_t)$ = all per-step probs multiplied
$\nabla_\theta$ (nabla) Gradient with respect to $\theta$ — "which direction is uphill?" $\nabla_\theta L$ = gradient of the loss
$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \theta_i}$ Partial derivative — same idea as gradient, one parameter at a time How much does $L$ change if I nudge $\theta_i$ slightly?
$\leftarrow$ Assignment — "update this value to be…" (not equals) $\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \nabla_\theta L$ = the gradient descent step
$\approx$ Approximately equal $-\log(0.002) \approx 6.21$

Now read the formula again — all at once

$$L = -\log\, P(\,y_{\text{correct}} \mid \text{context};\, \theta\,)$$

In plain English: "The loss equals the negative log of the probability that this model (with weights $\theta$) assigned to the correct next token, given all the previous tokens."

If the model is confident and correct — high $P$ → low $-\log P$ → small loss. If the model is wrong or uncertain — low $P$ → high $-\log P$ → large loss. Training pushes the loss down, which means training pushes the model to assign higher probability to correct tokens.

You now know enough to read most RLHF math

Every RLHF formula uses combinations of: $P(\cdot \mid \cdot;\,\theta)$, $\log$, $\mathbb{E}$, $\sum$, and $\nabla_\theta$. You have all of them above. When a new symbol appears, ask — but 90% of what you'll see is on this page.
